<div>Barry Kudrowitz, an assistant professor of product design at the University of Minnesota, talks Friday with a class of Purdue students about play in West Lafayette. (By Brent Drinkut/Journal &amp; Courier)</div>
<div>Creativity injected into engineering students&#8217; education through an innovative course, directed by Professor Ramani. (by jconline.com)</div>
<p>For four hours Friday evening, a group of about 70 Purdue University students and their teachers gathered at the school&#8217;s Discovery Learning Research Center.</p>
<p>Some might say they were there for child&#8217;s play &#8212; but not the kind you might think. The group had gathered for a seminar focusing on the engineering and design of children&#8217;s toys, everything from interactive dolls to card-based games.</p>
<p>Part of an elective mechanical engineering class, the seminar was a way to inject some creativity and innovation into the students&#8217; education, said Purdue mechanical engineer professor Karthik Ramani.</p>
<p>&#8220;Most engineers, like all people when they grow up, they lose the play,&#8221; he said. &#8220;We want to recreate that kind of divergent thinking and &#8230; build creativity and ingenuity into their thinking.&#8221;</p>
<p>Ramani created the class and has been teaching it since 1992, though Purdue professor David Anderson teaches the section that met Friday night.</p>
<p>The class runs in the fall and winter, and always fills to capacity, Ramani said.</p>
<p>He speculated that it&#8217;s popular because there&#8217;s a strong desire among future engineers to become more creative and innovative &#8212; traits that are likely to help their careers no matter their trajectory, he said.</p>
<p>&#8220;There&#8217;s a high chance that a lot of students will use this knowledge sometime in their careers in ways we&#8217;ll never know,&#8221; he said.</p>
<p>At the seminar Friday night, students heard from University of Minnesota professor Barry Kudrowitz, a toy designer whose master&#8217;s work &#8212; and subsequent classes he taught &#8212; were funded by toy giant Hasbro at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.</p>
<p>During the seminar, Kudrowitz showed the students the Atom Blaster &#8212; a NERF toy he designed that shoots foam balls.</p>
<p>In the second half of the seminar, the students, split into 20 groups, sketched potential toy designs. By semester&#8217;s end, those designs will turn into one working toy per group, Ramani said.</p>
<p>Several students said they took the class as a way to inject some creativity into their future.</p>
<p>&#8220;It&#8217;s definitely showing me different ways of looking at things, rather than the straight-forward way,&#8221; said Purdue senior Peter Stroh.</p>
