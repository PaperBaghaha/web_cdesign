<p>Professor Karthik Ramani was interviewed by Kenneth Wong of Digital Engineering 24/7 regarding the recent release of the <a href="https://engineering.purdue.edu/cdesign/wp/a-large-scale-annotated-mechanical-components-benchmark-for-classification-and-retrieval-tasks-with-deep-neural-networks/">Mechanical Component Benchmark dataset</a>.</p>
<p>You can listen to Professor Ramani&#8217;s interview on Digital Engineering&#8217;s web site: <a href="https://www.digitalengineering247.com/article/purdue-university-uses-machine-learning-to-classify-mechanical-objects/traceparts-inc#">https://www.digitalengineering247.com/article/purdue-university-uses-machine-learning-to-classify-mechanical-objects/traceparts-inc</a></p>
<blockquote><p>In early 2000, Dr. Karthik Ramani experimented with a shape-based search system that allows engineers to use visual references to identify and search for mechanical objects, but it soon became clear to him the computing power was insufficient.</p>
<p>Twenty years later, with far more computing power at his disposal, he revived the project, applying machine learning (ML) to crawl through a database of gearboxes, bearings, brakes, clutches, motors, nuts, bolts, and washers. The outcome is an open-source annotated database of more than 58,000 3D mechanical parts.</p></blockquote>
