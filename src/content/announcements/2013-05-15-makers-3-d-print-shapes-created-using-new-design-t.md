<p>WEST LAFAYETTE, Ind. Ã¢â‚¬â€œ A new design tool interprets hand gestures, enabling designers and artists to create and modify three-dimensional shapes using only their hands as a &#8220;natural user interface&#8221; instead of keyboard and mouse.</p>
<p>The tool, called Shape-It-Up, uses specialized computer algorithms and a depth-sensing camera to observe and interpret hand movements and gestures. The user creates shapes in a computer by interacting with a virtual workspace as the shape is displayed on a large-screen monitor.</p>
<p>&#8220;You create and modify shapes using hand gestures alone, no mouse or keyboard,&#8221; said Karthik Ramani, Purdue University&#8217;s Donald W. Feddersen Professor of Mechanical Engineering. &#8220;By bringing hands into the virtual space with a single depth camera we are able to manipulate the 3-D artifacts as if they actually exist.&#8221;</p>
<p>Researchers call the underlying technique shapeÃ¢â‚¬â€œgestureÃ¢â‚¬â€œcontext interplay. The tool could have applications in areas including games, architecture, art and engineering design, and also serves the emerging &#8220;creative maker&#8221; community, he said. The team will demonstrate the technology at the Maker Faire on Saturday and Sunday (May 18 and 19) in the San Mateo (Calif.) County Event Center.</p>
<p>&#8220;Our goal is to make the designer an integral part of the shape-modeling process during early design, which isn&#8217;t possible using current CAD tools,&#8221; Ramani said. &#8220;The conventional tools have non-intuitive and cognitively onerous processes requiring extensive training. We conclusively demonstrate the modeling of a wide variety of asymmetric 3-D shapes within a few seconds. One can bend and deform them in various ways to explore new shapes by natural interactions. <p>The creations can then be produced using a 3-D printer.</p>
<p>Research findings appeared in the February issue of Computer-Aided Design magazine. The paper was co-authored by Ramani, graduate students Vinayak and Sundar Murugappan and postdoctoral researcher HaiRong Liu. The paper is available at <a href="https://engineering.purdue.edu/cdesign/wp/?p=1571">https://engineering.purdue.edu/cdesign/wp/?p=1571</a></p>
<p>The research, funded by the National Science Foundation (NSF), addresses the limitations of conventional computer-aided design tools needed to create geometric shapes. Work to develop a model for transforming the research into market innovations was funded by the NSF&#8217;s Innovation Corps, or I-Corps, program and recently by NSF&#8217;s Accelerating Innovation Research (AIR).</p>
<p>The system harnesses the natural user interface of hand gestures to create and modify shapes.</p>
<p>&#8220;We are going from Windows icons, menus and pointers &#8211; or WIMPs &#8211; to a post-WIMP, natural user interface, or NUI,&#8221; Ramani said.</p>
<p>The tool is an advance over a previous version that was limited to creating &#8220;rotationally symmetric&#8221; objects, or those having the same measurements on all sides.</p>
<p>&#8220;This is important because many of the things designers need to create are not symmetrical,&#8221; Ramani said.</p>
<p>It uses the Microsoft Kinect camera, which senses three-dimensional space. The camera is found in consumer electronics games and can track a person&#8217;s body without using handheld electronics.</p>
<p>Researchers created advanced algorithms that recognize the hand gesture, understand that the hand is interacting with the shape and then modify the shape in response to the hand interaction. A video showing how the system works is available at <a href="http://youtu.be/JchASd9Yf_Y">http://youtu.be/JchASd9Yf_Y</a></p>
<p>The Purdue C Design Lab in the School of Mechanical Engineering is collaborating with a startup company, ZeroUI.</p>
